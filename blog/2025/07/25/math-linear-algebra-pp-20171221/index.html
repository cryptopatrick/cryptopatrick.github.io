1a)

Given two vectors $\mathbf{\vec{u}} = [1,2,2], \; \mathbf{\vec{v}}=[-1,2,5]$, find a third vector $|\mathbf{\vec{w}}| \gt 0$, which is orthogonal to both vectors $\mathbf{\vec{u}}$ and $\mathbf{\vec{v}}$.

Concept: The Dot Product

The dot product is a way to multiply two vectors and get a scalar value in return (a number, rather than another vector). â €â €â €â €â €The dot product can be geometrically interpreted as multiplying the lengths of Two vectors are orthogonal if their dotproduct is 0.

Example:
$$\mathbf{a} = [1,2,3], \; \mathbf{b}=[4,5,6], \; \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = (1\cdot4) + (2\cdot5) + (3\cdot6) := 4+10+18 = 32$$

We can write the dot product as, multiplying the magnitudes of two vectors by the angle $cos(\theta)$ between them: $$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|cos(\theta)$$

Concept: The Cross Product

We can find a vector $\mathbf{c} \in R^3$ which is orthogonal to two other vectors $a$ and $b$, by calculating their cross product, which is a binary operation on two vectors in $R^3$. The result of the operation, denoted $a \times b$ is new vector that is orthogonal (perpendicular) to the original vectors.

Example:
$$\mathbf{a} = [2,3,4], \; \mathbf{b}=[5,6,7],$$

$$ \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \begin{vmatrix} i & j & k \\ 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 5 & 6 & 7 \\ \end{vmatrix} $$ where $i,j,k$ are the _standard basis vectors for the $x,y,z$ axes. The calculation is done as follows:

$$ \begin{align} \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} &= \\ &= (3 \cdot 7 - 4 \cdot 6)i - (2 \cdot 7 - 4 \cdot 5)j + (2 \cdot 6 - 3 \cdot 5)k \\ &= (21-24)i - (14-20)j + (12-15)k \\ &= -3i + 6j - 3k \\ &= [-3,6,-3] \\ \end{align} $$

We can verify that our calculated vector $a \times b$ is indeed _orthogonal to $a$ and $b$, by using the dot product (remember, for orthogonality the result should be $0$):

$$ \begin{align} (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) \cdot \mathbf{a} &= \\ &= [-3,6,-3] \cdot [2,3,4] \\ &= (-3 \cdot 2) + (6 \cdot 3) + (-3 \cdot 4) \\ &= -6 + 18 -12 \\ &= 0 \\ \end{align} $$

We’ve shown that the vector $a$ is orthogonal to the cross product. Calculating $(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) \cdot \mathbf{b}$ will show the same.

Solution

We had two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$, and we wanted to find a third vector $\mathbf{c}$, which was orthogonal (perpendicular) to both these vectors in $R^3$ (three-dimensional space).
â €â €â €â €â € Using the cross product, we calculated vector $\mathbf{c}$. We then used the dot product to verify that it was indeed orthogonal to vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$.

1b)

For which values, of the parameter $a$, does the vectors $\mathbf{v}_1 = (a + 1,3))$ and $\mathbf{v}_2 = (2,-6)$ represent a basis in $R^2$?

Concept: Linear Independence

Two or more vectors are said to be linearly independent if we cannot create one of the vectors by combining the others. We can think of it this way, if a vector is seen as a path to a coordinate, then we cannot reach that coordinate by combining the paths of the other vectors that are linearly independet to that vector.

Concept: Vector Space

A set (collection) of vectors. We can add them together, and also multiply each vector by a scalar. For the set $A$ to be a vector space, the operations vector addition and scalar multiplication, must satisfy ten axioms. If these axioms are satisfied, then the “vectors” $\in A$ don’t have to be arrows in 2D or 3D space, but can be much more abstract objects, such as _matrices, polynomials, functions, or other mathematical objects.

Axioms: closure under addition and scalar multiplication, associativity, existance of a zero vector, every vector must have an additive inverse in the set, $$ \text{if } u,v \in V \rightarrow u+v \in V \\ \text{if } u \in V \text{ and } c \in R \rightarrow cv \in V\\ $$

Example:
$R^2$ is a vector space, that contains all the numbers, and they can be visualized as vectors on a 2D Cartesian plane. $\langle x,y \rangle$ has the additive inverse of $\langle -x,-y \rangle$, and zero vector $\langle 0,0 \rangle$.

Concept: Span

The span of a set of vectors is the set of all possible linear combinatiosn of those vectors. This gives us all the vectors that can be “reached” by scaling and adding the vectors in the set.
â €â €â €â €â € For a single vector, the span are all the points along that vector which we can reach by scaling the vector. For two non-parallel vectors, the span are all the points that are covered by the 2D-plane the vectors create. Any point on that plane can be reached by scaling xor adding the two vectors. For three non-coplanar vectors, the span is the entire 3D space.
â €â €â €â €â € A span is always a subspace of a vector space. Any vector in the vector space can be written as a unique combination (a linear combination) of the basis vectors: $\text{Span } \{v_1, v_2 \} = \{ c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 \, | \, c_1, c_2 \in R\}$

Concept: Basis

A basis is a set of vectors that can be used as building blocks for a vector space. We can think of it as the smallest possible set of vectors need to build the entire vector space. For the set of vectors to be used as a basis, it must satify two conditions:

  1. All the vectors that are members of the set must be linearly independent.
  2. The vectors must span the space.

Example:
A common example of a basis is the standard basis of the 2D Cartesioan plane, $R^2$. This plane has two axes, $\langle x, y \rangle$, and so our basis consists of the two vectors $i=\langle 1,0 \rangle$ and $j=\langle 0,1 \rangle$. We can check conditions for these to vectors $i$ and $j$ to be a basis:

  1. Linearly independet? Yes. We cannot multiply $i$ by any scalar to get $j$, and vice versa.
  2. Span? Yes. We can create any vector $w \in R^2$ by scalar multiplication of the basis. For exmaple $w = \langle 4,7 \rangle$ can be created by $w = 4i - 7j = 4 \langle 1,0 \rangle - 7 \langle 0,1 \rangle = \langle 4,-7 \rangle$.

We can check if a two vectors $\in R^2$ are linearly independent by calculating their determinant. If the determinant isn’t $0$, then we have a basis.

Solution:
So the technique is to form a matrix containing our two vectors, $\mathbf{v}_1 = (a + 1,3)$ and $\mathbf{v}_2 = (2,-6)$: by creating two colons of the vectors, and placing them into a matrix:

$$ \begin{align} A &= \begin{vmatrix} a + 1 & 3 \\ 2 & -6 \\ \end{vmatrix} \\ \end{align} $$

Remember, the two vectors $v_1$ and $v_2$ form a basis, unless $det(A) = 0$. So we need to calculate the value of $a$ where $det(A) = 0$. We start with the determinant of $A$.

$$ \begin{align} det(A) &= (a + 1)(-6) - (3)(2) \\ &= (-6a + -6) - 6 \\ &= -6a - 12 \\ \end{align} $$

Next we check the value of $a$ for which $det(A) = 0$: $$ -6a-12 = 0 \\ a = \frac{12}{-6} \\ a = -2 $$

Solution

The value $a=-2$ is the only one where the vectors $v_1$ and $v_2$ do not form a basis.

Concept: Parameterized Form

A line in $R^2$ can be written in the form: $$\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_0 + t \vec{v}$$ where $\vec{r}_0$ is a point on the line and $t \vec{v}$ is its direction vector.

We can find the intersection of two lines by rewriting them in parameterized form and solve for equality (s = t):

We rewrite the lines in parameterized form and get:
$$\text{1.) }(x,y): x=1+3t, y=2-t$$ $$\text{2.) }(x,y): x= 2+s, y=3-s$$

Next we want to solve for equality (1.x = 2.x and 1.y = 2.y):

  1. rewrite them as x_u = 1 + 3t, y_u = 2-t and x_v = 2+s, y_v = 3-s.
  2. set the x_u = x_v and y_u = y_v:
  3. solve: 1 + 3t = 2 + s := 3t =1 +s := s = 3t - 1
  4. replace s in: 2-t = 3-s: 2-t = 3-(3t -1) := 2-t = 4-3t := t = 1
  5. replace t=1 in first eq (1+3t = 2 + s): 1 + 3(1) = 2 + s := s = 2
  6. use t=1 in line 1 (u) to get intersection: (x,y) = (1,2) = 1*(3,-1) = (4,1)
